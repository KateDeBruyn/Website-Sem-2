Data Visualisation and Digital Information

The Field of Data Visualisation

To comprehend the world around us we use various methods of representation that help produce forms of knowledge for us to learn, interpret and apply. One of these methods of representation involves the visualisation of data - the science and practice of visually representing data as consumable, understandable information. Though definitions of data visualisation may be various, somewhat subjective and come from different angles. It has been defined as transformative, computerised, information design; all with an emphasis on its ability to be read and understood. It has been explained as a method to “present features of data with basic element of graph from various dimensions and perspectives” (Cheong Chiek Hua et al., 2018). It has been defined as “a transformation of quantified data which is not visual into a visual representation” (Manovich in Li 2020). The perspective I come from is understanding data visualisation as the methods used to enable 'raw' incomprehensible data to become comprehensive and learned. Murray (2017, p. 1) echoes this perspective as they believe “raw data only becomes useful only when we apply methods of deriving insight from it.”

Methodologies used to visually represent raw data involve the use of tables, charts, diagrams, graphs, simulations, waveforms, to name a few. These methods arise in two forms of the field: information and scientific visualisation. The design of information visualisation forefronts the communication of information whereas the scientific field focuses on using data to visualise phenomena (Li 2020). Information visualisation may use a graph to indicate previous waves of COVID19 infection rates over the last two years and scientific visualisation may simulate a heatmap of a country to indicate the transformation of hotspots during those waves. Both examples required quantifiable raw data to create a visual representation of what that data can mean and do. Data visualisation enables meaning to be derived from data.

Representing data visually comes with advantages, aspects and characteristics, and even supporting theories of perception, design and aesthetics. Advantages will be discussed at the end of this section because it opens the door to Data Visualisation and Web Development. According to Li (2020, pg 19), there are four aspects: real-time interaction, on-the-fly processing, visual scalability and user assistance and personalisation. The first refers to real-time techniques that support the response of vast collections of data that are interacted with. Secondly, it requires quick and supporting 'on-the-fly' visualisations. Scalability enables the vast and dynamic data sets to be added to and changed. And the user aspect of data visualisation speaks to the usability of the information - fields like UI and UX. Characteristics include readability, recognisability and meaning and they are “based on data communication for comprehension and knowledge acquisition” (Li 2020, p. 41). There are also two types of data. Primary and secondary data refer to types of data that are either collected directly from the source or extracted from already processed data - the first being primary and the second being secondary. It is important to understand the elements and angles that come with data visualisation so that we can make our visualisation techniques thorough, impactful, and meaningful.

There are many advantages to data visualisation and understanding the relationship between data and information. Li (2020, p. 21) also echoes Author's quote when saying, “data itself has no value, significance or meaning until it has been processed into a form of information.” In short, data, primary or secondary, can be understood as unprocessed information and information can be understood as processed data. The advantage of the relationship is that information is what gives data a 'voice'. We can study and learn proper ways to organise, portray and represent information gathered from data. When we learn better ways to do so, our information becomes meaningful and purposeful, and our techniques take full advantage of what data visualisation has to offer. Data visualisation has the benefit of its ability to represent huge amounts of data immediately and concisely and patterns, analysis, hypothesise, and insights can be derived and gathered from it instantly. It bleeds into the fields of User Interface and User Experience as visualisation techniques, like tables and heatmaps, are intrinsically linked to how the user can interact with and comprehend the processed data. UI and UX are fields found in field of Digital Information, which is where this blog would like to turn its attention to. The advantages of data visualisation extend from the ones mentioned here, though if there is one advantage to be keenly aware of in this scenario, it is that data visualisation under the use and practise of computer-based technology and techniques generates digital information and knowledge from data.

Data Visualisation and Digital Information

We are in the field (and era) of digital information and learning the best practices to represent information digitally is crucial to the role we play as knowledge producers. Representing information, in ways that include using data visualisation, is intrinsically linked to design metaphors. These metaphors are regarded as key elements, like concepts, terms, and images that represent data for a user to comprehend, consume, recall and command (Marcus, 1998, p. 129). We can see that design metaphors and data visualisation have a very close relationship and similar nature. We may always find one in the other.

To conclude, understanding what digital information entails helps us understand how we create, plan, code, and develop. In this case, we apply this to the websites we create. For example, if we used a graph to represent South Africa's COVID19 wave trends from 2020 to 2022, we would have to apply principles from data visualisation and design metaphors. Both play a part in how that graph is created and portrayed. We would use our understanding of graphs, taken from data visualisation, to organise and process the data into information. Design metaphors could then be applied to create a graph that follows traditional 'conditions' that deem it a graph - such as it will use at least an x and y axis. This may seem obvious to us now because most of us know what a graph it - but we learned what a graph is and how to use it because of the fields of data visualisation and design metaphors and their practice. Thus, when we practice data visualisation and use design metaphors, we are aiming to create information that is as identifiable and readable and as common as something like the graph.
