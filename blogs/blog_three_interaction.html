Defining and Understanding Interaction

Interaction in data visualisation has become an area of study in its own right that draws from the field of human-computer interaction (HCI). It is important to define and understand interaction as it plays a key role in visualisation design practices and enabled flexibility in visualisation design tools. Interaction is defined differently and mostly broad in both the visualisation research community and the HCI community. Furthermore, interaction's definition can be separated from interactivity, as well as interaction technique and the science of interaction (Dimara and Perin, 2020, p. 5) The two communities, visualisation research and HCI, are where Dimara and Perin draw their argument from in order to form an inclusive definition for what interaction can be understood as. In the interactive pie chart below are some summarising points to the concepts Dimara and Perin base their research on.

The purpose of the pie chart is to demonstrate not only how broad the definition for interaction may be, but to highlight how various parts of the definition weigh differently depending on any given scenario. The ideas of interaction as a mediating entity, or human intent, as a dialogue, or as an amplification of cognition, are derived from the visualisation view. Whereas the HCI view sees interaction as a transmission of information, a cycle of communication, actions with a start, end and response, or a causal relationship. Both views include various definitions within themselves which is why I chose to represent this multi-faceted concept of interaction in a chart that shifts the weight of each definition. The shift in weight, enabled by the user's click of the button, represents how some definitions may be more applicable and appropriate than others depending on the kind of interaction one may be observing.

In the visualisation research community, Dimara and Perin (2020, p. 6) found that overarching definition for interaction is “a dialogue between a human user and the visualization system over a central object of interest: the data”. There are also three approaches; system-, task-, and human-centric, that describe the concept of interaction within the field of visualisation. This is where interaction intends to amplify cognition during a person's exploration of data. It is important to note that the person, the user, is also “in control of the information space” (Dimara and Perin, 2020, p. 5). Some of the properties the community assigns interaction with include granularity (micro and macro actions), continuous interactions versus discrete, and degree of directness and indirectness. The issues that lie within the visualisation community's definition of interaction is that the terms used to describe interaction are rarely agreed upon. However, the authors do offer a compacted definition and describe interaction for visualisation as “the interplay between a person and a data interface involving a data-related intent, at least one action from the person and an interface reaction that is perceived as such” (Dimara and Perin, 2020, p. 8).

The HCI view includes seven concepts that systematise what the community believes as good interaction. These include dialogue (where the cycle of communication definition comes in), transmission, control, tool use, optimal behaviour, embodiment and experience. Dialogue involves the cycle of communitive actions; transmission sees the transmittal of information between users and computers; control defines interaction in systems consisted of goals, inputs and outputs, responses and states; tools refer to those that are used to enable action; behaviour sees interaction as an adaptive behaviour; embodiment relates to the action of being in the world and participation; and finally, experience defines interaction as phenomena shaped by a user's expectation, reaction and recall (Dimara and Perin 2020, p. 6).
