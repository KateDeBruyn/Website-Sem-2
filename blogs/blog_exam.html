Data Visualisation, Data Art and Representation

Introduction

The fields of Art and Data Visualisation intersect with one another, and create a relationship, when an artwork is formed by using raw data, its information, and its data visualisation techniques. This essay will discuss the intricacies in these relationships and how the theories of representation are embedded in the process. Representation cannot be divorced from data visualisation and data art, because the two fields rely on representational theories to form themselves and give their form meaning. First, the relationship between data visualisation and art will be discussed, and then the discourse will move over to the role representation plays.

Data Visualisation and Data Art

Data Art can be termed and understood as “data-driven” art; art that uses and is based on raw data that has been converted into information through data visualisation techniques. Data art encapsulates the intersectional relationship between art and the science of data visualisation. Data-driven artwork, as opposed to broader art, can be identified by its “intent rather than surface aesthetics” (Viégas and Wattenberg, 2007, p. 3) because the artwork's intent is to represent the raw data and its information. To do this, the artwork may make use of data visualisation techniques which also show the conversion of raw data into information that a viewer can gain meaning from. Data art and data visualisation have an interdependent relationship with one another when it comes to finding ways to represent the data best.

A more specific field of art can be used to examine this relationship further - Techno-Art. Sabine Roeser (2016, p. 127) coined this term and used it to describe “art that reflects on and engages with risky technologies”. An example of this could be the Last Clock data-driven artwork by Jussi Ängeslevä and Ross Cooper. Last Clock is made up of video footage warped into three concentric circles to form and represent a clock. The data used is digital video data; encoded digital images that render in rapid succession to form a video. This process of electronic representation, the video itself, could be argued as the data visualisation technique. Ängeslevä and Cooper used and based their artwork on this technique and technology. Firstly, the interdependent relationship can be identified in their use of a clock to represent video footage that renders a current recording of the camera's surroundings. Both the clock and the video recording constitute and symbolise time, and furthermore, the clock uses the current video recordings to symbolise time and the video uses the clock to record and render time passing. Secondly, while video recording may not be regarded as a risky technology that art needs to reflect on, for the sake of this aspect I introduce Jeremy Bentham's Panopticon.

The Panopticon was designed by Bentham to illustrate systems of control and theories of self-surveillance. It is a circular prison comprised of cells that line the walls with a guard tower (or inspector's office) in the centre of the building. All the cells, a prisoner in each, can see and be observed by the middle office. However, they cannot see whether there is an inspector in the office. Bentham (1791) describes this as “the sentiment of a sort of invisible omnipresence.” Assuming they are always being watched, the prisoners become self-surveying of their behaviour and actions. Can we not liken this to public video cameras (like security cameras) that make us conscious of the other who may be watching? We are always able to see these cameras, and sometimes even told via signage, but we never see the inspector. Does this not then feel like a risky technology? Something capable of inducing scopophobia in the victim? In this regard, Last Clock can be seen as techno-art as it can offer a space for “ethical and political thinking… ethical reflection… [and] probe emotional-moral reflection” (Roeser, 2016, p. 128 - 129); which are intrinsic characteristics of techno-art. This Panopticon aspect of Last Clock also ties in representation to the relationship between data art and visualisation.

Strategies of Representation

The discourse around panopticism, representation, data art and visualisation need to be guided by strong theories of representation. Michel Foucault examined Bentham's Panopticon and applied its theory to the representations of power. Before linking strategies of representation in data art and visualisation to power, representation itself needs to be defined.

Stuart Hall (1997, p. 16) defines representation as "the production of meaning through language". Language is a blanket term for any system of communication. Written text, imagery, or symbolism can be languages. The code used to program data visualisations and art is a language. Language aids the construction of meaning, with which representation is concerned (Kidd, 2015, p. 19). In the visual form of communication, where data visualisation and art can be found, language operates differently and sometimes disadvantageously. According to Kidd, visual communication can be “ambiguous, suggestive, and open to multiple interpretations” (2015, p. 26 - 27) because it is not underpinned by spoken language and is not always contextualised; its denotative and connotative meanings can differ to various viewers and often do. This particularly occurs in the case of Hall's constructivist approach. The constructivist approach posits representation as the “active labour of makings things mean” (Hall in Kidd 2015, p. 23). Everybody has a different way of doing this which is why art can spark various emotional responses and different discussions. While data visualisation practices aim to avoid anything vague, data-driven art uses data visualisation and representation strategies to invoke abstract feelings, opinions, and questions. Last Clock can be, and has been, analysed in this essay through the lens of data-driven art, visualisation, representation and even panopticism, but at the end of the day this is based on my own constructivist approach. Last Clock may have an entirely different meaning to another viewer as well as Ängeslevä and Cooper themselves.

Data visualisation and data-driven art and their relationship engage with representational strategies like the constructivist approach. Data-art's active labour is in making raw data have meaning through data visualisation, artistic practice, and representation. But how does this labour relate to power? Does it? In the case of Last Clock and its panoptic quality, the answer is yes. Foucault describes the effect of the Panopticon as one that induces “in the inmate a state of conscious and permanent visibility that assures the automatic functioning of power” (1975, p.3). The inmate is permanently subjected to the power the inspector holds through observation. Last Clock functions similarly to the Panopticon, in terms of constant surveillance, and is thus a powerful data-driven artwork. Ängeslevä and Cooper representational strategy, on the surface, was to use video footage and a clock. However, on a deeper level, their strategy is one of panoptic power as the representation of the video footage and ever-ticking clock implies the permanent surveillance.

Conclusions

This is not to say that these are the only representational strategies out there that underpin the relationship between data visualisation and art. The example of the Last Clock is to deeply analyse what the relationship and representational strategies can mean. What artists can be capable of, accidently or intentionally, invoking in their viewers. Representation plays a role in the artist's decision making, not only in their question of how best to present the data they are using but also in their questions to “what does this mean?” and “how do the viewers make meaning?”. Analysing Last Clock is an example of this of itself. I am a viewer who used my previous knowledge, experiences, and opinions to formulate the meaning of Last Clock, whether they were close to the intentions of the artists or not.

Representation strategies and understanding them is at the forefront of analysing the relationship between data visualisation and data-driven art. Even strategies such as Gestalt Principles play a role in the creation of data-driven art, and thus play a role in its relationship to data visualisation. It may be wrong to not mention a representational strategy such as that because it is so intrinsic to design. And design is so intrinsic to art and data visualisation.

In expressing an artistic intent or form, data-based creative practices synthesize and build upon techniques in communications and aesthetics.
